What is Bitcoin?
Beginner
▼

Bitcoin (BTC) is the world's first decentralized digital currency, created in 2009 by an anonymous person or group known as Satoshi Nakamoto. It operates without any central authority — no bank, no government controls it.

  • Fixed supply: Only 21 million BTC will ever exist — making it deflationary by design
  • Decentralized: Thousands of computers worldwide maintain the network simultaneously
  • Transparent: Every transaction is publicly recorded on the blockchain forever
  • Borderless: Send value to anyone anywhere in minutes with no middleman

How Does Blockchain Work?
Beginner
▼

A blockchain is a chain of data blocks linked together cryptographically. Each block contains a list of transactions, a timestamp, and a reference to the previous block — making it virtually impossible to alter history.

  • Immutable: Once data is written, it cannot be changed without redoing all subsequent blocks
  • Distributed: Copies exist on thousands of nodes — no single point of failure
  • Consensus: Nodes must agree on the state of the ledger (Proof of Work or Proof of Stake)
  • Transparent: Anyone can audit the full transaction history at any time
💡 Think of it like this: A blockchain is like a Google Doc that thousands of people have a copy of — but nobody can secretly edit the old pages.
🔐 Hash ⛏️ Proof of Work 🪙 Proof of Stake 🌐 Node

Crypto Wallets Explained
Beginner
▼

A crypto wallet doesn't actually store your coins — it stores your private keys, which prove ownership of assets on the blockchain. Think of your wallet address like a bank account number, and your private key like the PIN.

  • Hot wallet: Connected to the internet (MetaMask, Trust Wallet) — convenient but less secure
  • Cold wallet: Offline hardware device (Ledger, Trezor) — most secure for large amounts
  • Custodial: Exchange holds your keys (Binance, Coinbase) — convenient but "not your keys, not your coins"
  • Non-custodial: You control your own keys — full ownership, full responsibility
⚠️ NEVER share your seed phrase with anyone — not support teams, not friends, not anyone. Anyone with your seed phrase owns your wallet.
🔑 Private Key 🌱 Seed Phrase 📬 Public Address 🧊 Cold Storage

Market Cap & Crypto Rankings
Beginner
▼

Market capitalization is the most common way to rank cryptocurrencies. It measures the total value of all coins in circulation — not just the price per coin.

  • Formula: Market Cap = Price × Circulating Supply
  • Large cap ($10B+): Bitcoin, Ethereum — more stable, less risky
  • Mid cap ($1B–$10B): More growth potential, more volatile
  • Small cap (under $1B): High risk, high reward — many fail
💡 Example: A coin at $0.001 with 1 trillion supply has a $1B market cap — bigger than a $100 coin with 1 million supply ($100M market cap). Price alone is misleading!
📈 Market Cap 🔄 Circulating Supply 💎 FDV 📉 Dominance

What is DeFi?
Intermediate
▼

Decentralized Finance (DeFi) refers to financial services built on public blockchains — lending, borrowing, trading, earning yield — all without banks or brokers. Smart contracts replace middlemen automatically.

  • Lending: Earn interest by supplying assets to protocols like Aave or Compound
  • Borrowing: Take loans using crypto as collateral — no credit checks
  • Yield Farming: Provide liquidity and earn trading fees + token rewards
  • Staking: Lock tokens to secure networks and earn passive income

DEX vs CEX — What's the Difference?
Intermediate
▼

There are two main types of crypto exchanges. Centralized (CEX) are run by companies. Decentralized (DEX) run on smart contracts with no company in control.

  • CEX (Binance, Coinbase): Easy to use, fast, but they hold your funds and require KYC
  • DEX (Uniswap, PancakeSwap): You keep your keys, no sign-up, but harder to use
  • AMM: DEXes use Automated Market Makers — prices set by liquidity pool ratios, not order books
  • Slippage: Large trades on DEXes move the price — small liquidity pools = higher slippage
💡 Pro tip: Use CEX for large, established coins. Use DEX to access new tokens early — but always check for honeypots with our Rug Checker first!
🔄 AMM 📉 Slippage ⛽ Gas Fees 🆔 KYC

Liquidity Pools & How They Work
Intermediate
▼

Liquidity pools are smart contracts holding two tokens that allow anyone to trade between them. Instead of matching buyers and sellers like traditional exchanges, prices are calculated automatically based on the ratio of tokens in the pool.

  • Liquidity Providers (LPs): Users who deposit equal values of two tokens to earn trading fees
  • Trading fees: Typically 0.3% per trade, distributed to LPs proportionally
  • Impermanent loss: When token prices diverge, LPs can end up with less value than just holding
  • Pool depth: More liquidity = less price impact for large trades
⚠️ Impermanent Loss: If you provide ETH/USDC liquidity and ETH doubles in price, you would have earned more by just holding ETH. Always calculate IL before providing liquidity.

How to Spot Crypto Scams
Beginner
▼

Crypto scams cost billions every year. Knowing the red flags can save your funds. Scammers are sophisticated — they impersonate real projects, celebrities, and even support staff.

  • Rug pulls: Devs launch a token, hype it, then drain the liquidity pool overnight
  • Phishing: Fake websites mimicking real ones — always check the exact URL
  • Fake giveaways: "Send 1 ETH, get 2 back" — always a scam, always
  • Pump & dump: Coordinated groups hype a token then sell on retail buyers
  • Honeypots: Tokens you can buy but never sell — our Rug Checker catches these
🚨 Golden Rule: If it sounds too good to be true — guaranteed returns, celebrity endorsements, urgent limited-time offers — it IS a scam. No exceptions.
🎣 Phishing 🪤 Honeypot 🏃 Rug Pull 📈 Pump & Dump

How to Secure Your Crypto
Beginner
▼

Security in crypto is entirely your responsibility. Unlike a bank, there is no customer support to recover lost funds. One mistake can mean permanent loss. Here's how to protect yourself:

  • Hardware wallet: Store large amounts on Ledger or Trezor — offline and safe
  • Seed phrase backup: Write it on paper, store in multiple secure locations. Never digital
  • Use 2FA: Always enable authenticator app 2FA on exchanges — never SMS
  • Verify contracts: Before interacting with any token, check it on our Rug Checker
  • Revoke approvals: Regularly revoke unused token approvals at revoke.cash
✅ Best practice: Use a separate "burner" wallet for DeFi experiments. Keep your main holdings in a cold wallet that never touches DeFi protocols.
🧊 Hardware Wallet 🔑 2FA ✅ Token Approval 🔥 Burner Wallet
🛡️
Reading a Token Contract Safely
Intermediate
▼

Before buying any new token — especially on DEXes — you should always analyze the smart contract. Most rug pulls and honeypots have clear warning signs in the contract code.

  • Honeypot check: Use our built-in Rug Checker to test if a token can be sold
  • Sell tax: Taxes over 10% on sells are a major red flag — some go up to 99%
  • Ownership renounced? If the owner can still mint tokens or change taxes, it's risky
  • Liquidity locked? Check if LP tokens are burned or locked — prevents instant rug pulls
  • Holder concentration: If 1 wallet holds 50%+ of supply, a single dump can crash the price
